# Particulars of Claim

Brightmarket Retail GmbH  v  Cobalt Cloud Analytics Ltd
Claim No. 2026/0417 COM  |  Commercial Court (Ireland) - applying EU law

The Claimant ("Brightmarket") is a company incorporated in Germany which operates online retail stores across several EU member states and is the controller of its customers' personal data.

The Defendant ("Cobalt") supplies a cloud analytics platform and acted as the Claimant's processor under a Data Processing Agreement.

By a Master Subscription Agreement, a Data Processing Agreement and a Statement of Work each dated 1 March 2017 the Claimant engaged the Defendant to host and analyse its customer and transaction data.

Before contract the Defendant represented that the Platform was ISO 27001 certified and fully GDPR-compliant by design. That representation was false.

The Defendant processed the Claimant's customer personal data for its own product and model-training purposes without the Claimant's instruction.

On or about 10 October 2025 a personal-data breach exposed the personal data of approximately 2.3 million of the Claimant's customers.

Further or alternatively, even if fewer records were exfiltrated, at least approximately 1.9 million records were exposed within the meaning of Article 4(12) of the GDPR.

The Defendant failed to notify the relevant supervisory authority of the breach within 72 hours as required.

The breach was caused solely by the Defendant's failure to implement appropriate technical and organisational measures.

The Defendant's engineers left a production database publicly accessible on the internet for approximately three weeks before the breach.

The Platform was not in conformity with the agreed specification: the analytics export module produced materially inaccurate revenue figures.

The Defendant transferred the Claimant's data to a sub-processor in the United States in breach of the data-transfer restrictions of Directive 95/46/EC as incorporated by the Data Processing Agreement.

Further or alternatively, the transfers to the United States sub-processor were unlawful under Chapter V of the GDPR because the standard contractual clauses were not supplemented by adequate measures following the Schrems II judgment.

The Defendant failed to maintain adequate backups, so the Claimant's data could not be restored after the breach.

Further or alternatively, a subset of the Claimant's customer data was permanently lost in the breach and could not be reconstructed.

The Claimant has suffered loss, namely wasted subscription fees of EUR 900,000 and loss of profit and further exposure of EUR 6,000,000.

The Defendant's 2024 acquisition of the Claimant's previous supplier, NorthStar, was implemented without the required merger clearance, and the Defendant thereafter degraded interoperability to lock the Claimant in.

The Defendant deployed an automated forecasting system that made decisions producing legal or similarly significant effects on the Claimant's customers without the safeguards and transparency required by law.

The Defendant caused non-essential analytics and tracking cookies to be set on the Claimant's storefront without the consent required by Article 5(3) of the ePrivacy Directive.

The Defendant failed to assist the Claimant with, and itself failed to action, the Claimant's customers' data-subject access and erasure requests.

The Defendant warranted that the Platform would achieve 99.9% uptime, and is liable in damages for failing to meet that uptime warranty.

The October 2025 outage lasted 30 days and, at EUR 150,000 of lost margin per day, caused the Claimant EUR 4,500,000 in lost profit.

On 25 September 2025, before the breach, the Claimant warned the Defendant of the vulnerability, and the Defendant ignored that warning and failed to remediate it.

The Defendant unlawfully transferred the personal data of EU customers to its sub-processor in India in October 2025.

Under the Data Processing Agreement the Defendant gave an unlimited, uncapped indemnity for any data-protection breach.

The Defendant's encryption fell below the standard required by Article 32 GDPR because it used a deprecated cipher.

The Defendant failed to carry out the data protection impact assessment required by Article 35 GDPR.
